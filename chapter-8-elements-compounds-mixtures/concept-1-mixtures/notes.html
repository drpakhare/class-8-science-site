Chapter 8 · Concept 1

Mixtures

When substances mix but keep their own properties, you have a mixture — sometimes you can see the parts, sometimes you can't.

Key Concept What a mixture is

When two or more substances are mixed and each keeps its own properties (no chemical reaction), the result is a mixture. The substances in it are its components.

Non-uniform (heterogeneous)Uniform (homogeneous)
Components are visible / unevenly spreadComponents evenly spread, can't be seen separately
Sprout salad, sand in waterSugar in water, air
Alloys are uniform mixtures of metals: stainless steel (iron, nickel, chromium, carbon), brass (copper + zinc), bronze (copper + tin). Ancient India called such mixtures Mishraloha.

Air is a mixture

Air is a uniform mixture — mainly nitrogen (~78%), oxygen, argon, carbon dioxide and water vapour. Lime water turns milky in air, showing carbon dioxide is present. Suspended dust particles are pollutants, not a true part of air.

Key idea: in a mixture, components do not react and can usually be separated by physical methods.

Common Traps Where students lose marks

Trap 1 — Uniform vs non-uniform. Uniform = evenly mixed, parts not visible (sugar solution, air). Non-uniform = parts visible/uneven (salad, sand in water).
Trap 2 — Components keep their properties. In a mixture there is no chemical reaction; each substance stays itself.
Trap 3 — Air is a mixture, not a compound. Its gases are simply mixed (uniformly), not chemically combined.
Lime water test: turns milky with carbon dioxide (forms calcium carbonate).

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. Classify as uniform or non-uniform: (i) sugar solution (ii) sand in water.
Step 1:Sugar solution — evenly mixed, parts not visible → uniform.
Step 2:Sand in water — parts visible → non-uniform.
∴ (i) uniform (ii) non-uniform.
Example B. How does lime water show air contains carbon dioxide?
Step 1:Lime water (calcium hydroxide) left in air turns milky.
Step 2:CO₂ reacts with it to form insoluble calcium carbonate.
∴ The milkiness proves carbon dioxide is present in air.
