Laws of Reflection

Light bounces off any mirror by two simple rules — and a mirror's curve decides whether rays converge or diverge.

Key Concept The two laws

The ray hitting the mirror is the incident ray; the one bouncing off is the reflected ray; the line at 90° to the mirror at the point of incidence is the normal.

Angle of incidence (i) = angle of reflection (r).
1The angle of incidence (i) = angle of reflection (r).
2The incident ray, the normal, and the reflected ray all lie in the same plane.

These laws hold for all mirrors — plane, concave and convex.

Parallel rays on curved mirrors

•Plane mirror: parallel rays stay parallel after reflection.
•Concave mirror: parallel rays converge (meet) — can concentrate sunlight to burn paper.
•Convex mirror: parallel rays diverge (spread out).
Note: each ray still obeys i = r; the curve of the mirror is what makes the beam converge or diverge.

Common Traps Where students lose marks

Trap 1 — Angles are measured from the NORMAL, not the mirror surface. i and r are between the ray and the normal.
Trap 2 — Concave converges, convex diverges. Don't swap them.
Trap 3 — If a ray hits along the normal, i = r = 0° and it reflects straight back.
Both laws apply to spherical mirrors too — the curve only changes the overall beam, not the rule per ray.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. A ray hits a mirror at an angle of incidence of 35°. What is the angle of reflection?
Step 1:By the first law, i = r.
∴ Angle of reflection = 35°.
Example B. Why can a concave mirror burn paper in sunlight?
Step 1:It converges the parallel rays of the Sun to a point.
Step 2:The concentrated light gives enough heat to ignite the paper.
∴ Converging sunlight concentrates heat, burning the paper.
