Chapter 10 · Concept 1

Spherical Mirrors

Curve a mirror inward or outward and the image changes — that's a concave or convex mirror at work.

Key Concept Concave and convex mirrors

Mirrors with a spherical (curved) reflecting surface are spherical mirrors — like the two sides of a shiny spoon.

Concave mirrorConvex mirror
Reflecting surface curves inwardReflecting surface curves outward
Close object → enlarged, erect; far object → invertedAlways erect & diminished (smaller)

Where they are used

•Concave: torch/headlight reflectors, dentist's mirror (enlarged view of teeth), reflecting telescopes, solar concentrators.
•Convex: vehicle side-view mirrors, road-safety mirrors at bends, shop surveillance mirrors — they give an erect image and a wide view.
"Objects are closer than they appear": a convex side mirror makes vehicles look small and far, so the warning reminds you they're actually nearer.
Compare with a plane mirror: a plane mirror always gives an erect image of the same size; spherical mirrors change the size (and concave can invert it).

Common Traps Where students lose marks

Trap 1 — Concave curves IN, convex curves OUT. Concave = "caved in".
Trap 2 — Convex always gives erect, diminished images. Concave can give enlarged or inverted images depending on distance.
Trap 3 — Match mirror to use. Wide view/safety → convex; concentrate light or enlarge → concave.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. Why are convex mirrors used as side-view mirrors in vehicles?
Step 1:A convex mirror always forms an erect, diminished image.
Step 2:Because it curves outward, it covers a wider area behind.
∴ They give an upright, wide view of traffic behind.
Example B. Which mirror does a dentist use to see an enlarged image of a tooth, and why?
Step 1:An enlarged, erect image of a close object is needed.
Step 2:A concave mirror gives this when the object is close.
∴ A concave mirror, held close to the tooth.
