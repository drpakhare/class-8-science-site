Answer Key — Contact Forces: Muscular Force and Friction

Final answer with a one-line reason. Full methods are in the Notes' worked examples.

Section A Objective

Q1
(b) contact force — contact forces need physical touch.
Q2
(b) opposite to the direction of motion — friction opposes the motion of the object.
Q3
(b) rough surface — rougher surfaces have more friction.
Q4
(b) muscular force — these use the muscular force of our body.
Q5
(a) Both true, R explains A — friction needs two touching surfaces, so it is a contact force.
Q6
irregularities — tiny surface irregularities cause friction.
Q7
streamlined — streamlined shapes reduce fluid friction.

Section B Short answer

Q8
A force that acts only when objects are in physical contact; examples: muscular force and friction.
Q9
The force that acts when an object moves or tries to move over another surface; it acts opposite to the direction of motion.
Q10
On the nature of the surfaces in contact; friction is greater on rough surfaces and less on smooth ones.

Section C Long / application

Q11
Friction opposes the box's motion and stops it. Different surfaces have different roughness, so friction differs: it is least on smooth glass (box slides farthest) and most on rough sand (box stops soonest).
Q12
It arises from irregularities of two surfaces in contact, so it needs touch — a contact force. Helpful: lets us walk/grip without slipping. Nuisance: slows moving vehicles and wears out parts (any reasonable examples).
