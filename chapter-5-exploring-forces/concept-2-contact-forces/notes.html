Contact Forces: Muscular Force and Friction

Forces that act only when objects touch — the muscular force we use and the friction that opposes motion.

Key Concept Forces that need touch

Forces that act only when there is physical contact between objects are contact forces. (Contact can be direct — a hand — or indirect — a stick or rope.)

Muscular force

The force from the action of our muscles (walking, lifting, pushing) is muscular force. It works as muscles contract and relax. It also runs body processes — chewing, pushing food along the alimentary canal, and the heart pumping blood. Animals also use muscular force.

Friction

A rolling ball stops on its own because of friction. Friction is the force that acts when an object moves, or tries to move, over another surface. It always acts opposite to the direction of motion. It is a contact force.

•Why it happens: every surface has tiny irregularities; they lock into each other and oppose sliding.
•What it depends on: the nature of the surfaces — friction is greater on rough surfaces, less on smooth ones.
Friction in fluids: air and water also exert friction. That's why aeroplanes, ships and fast trains have streamlined shapes to reduce it.

Common Traps Where students lose marks

Trap 1 — Friction opposes motion. It always acts opposite to the direction the object moves or tries to move — never in the same direction.
Trap 2 — Friction is a contact force. It needs two surfaces touching; it isn't a "non-contact" force.
Trap 3 — Rough = more friction. A rougher surface gives more friction; a smoother one gives less.
Even fluids: air and water cause friction, so vehicles are streamlined.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. Why does a ball rolling on the ground slow down and stop?
Step 1:The surfaces of the ball and ground are in contact.
Step 2:Friction acts opposite to motion, reducing the speed until it stops.
∴ Friction opposes its motion and brings it to rest.
Example B. Why does the same box slide a shorter distance on sand than on glass?
Step 1:Sand is rougher than glass.
Step 2:Rougher surfaces give more friction.
∴ More friction on sand stops the box sooner.
