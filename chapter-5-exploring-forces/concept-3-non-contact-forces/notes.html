Chapter 5 · Concept 3

Non-contact Forces

Forces that act from a distance, without touching — magnetic, electrostatic and gravitational.

Key Concept Forces that act at a distance

Forces whose effect is felt even when objects are not in contact are non-contact forces. There are three to know.

Magnetic force

The force a magnet exerts on another magnet or a magnetic material is magnetic force. Like poles repel (N–N, S–S); unlike poles attract (N–S). It can act from a distance — so it is non-contact.

Electrostatic force

Rubbing certain materials builds up static charges on them — the object becomes charged. A charged object can attract light uncharged objects (paper bits) and other charges from a distance.

•There are two kinds of charge: positive and negative.
•Like charges repel; unlike charges attract.

Gravitational force (gravity)

The Earth pulls every object towards itself — the gravitational force, or gravity. It acts without contact, so it is non-contact, and it is always attractive (it only pulls, never pushes). That is why a thrown ball always falls back down.

Key contrast: magnetic and electrostatic forces can attract or repel; gravity is always attractive.

Common Traps Where students lose marks

Trap 1 — Like repel, unlike attract. True for both magnetic poles and electric charges. Don't reverse it.
Trap 2 — Gravity is always attractive. Unlike magnetic/electrostatic forces, it never repels.
Trap 3 — All three act without contact. Magnetic, electrostatic and gravitational forces are non-contact forces.
Charging: both the rubbing and rubbed objects get charged, but with opposite kinds of charge.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. Two balloons rubbed with wool move apart. Why?
Step 1:Both balloons get the same kind of static charge.
Step 2:Like charges repel.
∴ The similarly charged balloons repel and move apart.
Example B. Why does a thrown ball always come back down?
Step 1:The Earth exerts a gravitational pull on the ball.
Step 2:Gravity is always attractive, pulling the ball towards Earth.
∴ Gravity pulls it back to the ground.
