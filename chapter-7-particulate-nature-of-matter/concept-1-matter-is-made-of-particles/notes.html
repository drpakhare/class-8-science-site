Chapter 7 · Concept 1

Matter Is Made of Particles

Break chalk, dissolve sugar — both show matter is made of huge numbers of tiny particles with spaces between them.

Key Concept The building blocks of matter

If we keep breaking chalk — snapping, then grinding — we reach the smallest units it is made of. These tiny basic units are the constituent particles. One piece of chalk contains a huge number of them.

Grinding chalk is a physical change: the chalk stays chalk — only the size of each speck reduces.

Dissolving sugar

Stir sugar into water and it disappears, yet the water tastes sweet everywhere. The sugar broke into its constituent particles, which are too small to see (even under an ordinary microscope) and slipped into the gaps between the water particles.

•The gaps between particles are called interparticle spaces.
•The water level barely rises because sugar particles fit into these spaces.
Scientific heritage: the ancient Indian philosopher Acharya Kanad proposed that matter is made of tiny indivisible particles called Parmanu (in his Vaisheshika Sutras).

Common Traps Where students lose marks

Trap 1 — Dissolving sugar is a physical change. The sugar isn't destroyed; it splits into particles spread through the water (you can taste it).
Trap 2 — "Can't see it" doesn't mean "gone". Particles too small to see are still there — sense them by taste, smell, etc.
Trap 3 — Interparticle spaces are the gaps, not the particles. Sugar fits into the spaces between water particles.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. Why does the water level hardly rise when sugar dissolves in it?
Step 1:Sugar breaks into very tiny constituent particles.
Step 2:These fit into the interparticle spaces between water particles.
∴ The sugar occupies existing gaps, so the level barely changes.
Example B. Is grinding chalk into powder a physical or chemical change? Why?
Step 1:Grinding only reduces the size of each speck.
Step 2:No new substance forms — it is still chalk.
∴ It is a physical change.
