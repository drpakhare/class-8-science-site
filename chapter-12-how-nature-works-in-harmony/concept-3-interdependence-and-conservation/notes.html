Interdependence and Conservation

Nature stays in balance through interdependence — and human actions can disturb or protect it.

Key Concept Everything is connected

Living things depend on non-living things and on each other for nutrition, respiration and reproduction. Disturbing one part affects the whole habitat — this is interdependence.

•Plants make food using sunlight, air, water and soil; animals depend on plants and other animals.
•Removing one species (e.g. overfishing) can change the numbers of many others — a ripple effect.

Humans and habitats

Cutting forests for roads and buildings, and changes in rainfall and temperature, shrink animals' habitats. Losing their home, animals like elephants wander into farms and villages, causing conflict.

Wildlife corridors: ecologists mark corridors that connect forest patches, letting elephants and other animals move safely between forests without entering human settlements.
Take-away: protecting habitats and their balance protects all the life (including us) that depends on them.

Common Traps Where students lose marks

Trap 1 — Removing one species affects many. Food and pollination links mean a single change ripples through the habitat.
Trap 2 — Human-wildlife conflict comes from habitat loss. Elephants enter farms because their forest food/water is gone, not "by nature".
Trap 3 — A corridor connects habitats. It allows safe animal movement, reducing conflict — it is not a road for humans.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. Why do elephants sometimes enter farms and villages?
Step 1:Forests shrink due to tree-cutting and changes in rainfall/temperature.
Step 2:With food and water scarce in their habitat, elephants search nearby farms.
∴ Habitat loss pushes elephants into human areas.
Example B. How do wildlife corridors reduce human-elephant conflict?
Step 1:Corridors connect separate forest areas.
Step 2:Animals can move between forests safely without crossing villages.
∴ They give animals a safe path, reducing conflict.
