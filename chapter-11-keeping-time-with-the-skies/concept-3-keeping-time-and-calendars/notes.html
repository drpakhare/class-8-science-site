Keeping Time: Day, Month, Year and Calendars

The spinning Earth gives the day, the Moon's cycle the month, and Earth's orbit the year — the basis of every calendar.

Key Concept Natural cycles become units of time

UnitNatural cycleDuration
DayEarth's rotation on its axis~24 hours (mean solar day)
MonthMoon's cycle of phases (revolution around Earth)~29.5 days
YearEarth's revolution around the Sun (one cycle of seasons)~365¼ days

The shadow clock

The Sun is highest in the sky at midday, when shadows are shortest. The time from one such highest point to the next is the mean solar day (~24 hours). A sundial uses a shadow to tell time.

Calendars

•Lunar calendar — based on the Moon's phases; ~12 lunar months fit in one cycle of seasons.
•Solar calendar — based on the year (Earth's revolution); the one we commonly use.
•Many Indian calendars are lunisolar — they combine the Moon's months with the solar year.
Why a leap year? A year is about 365¼ days, so we add an extra day (Feb 29) every 4 years to keep the calendar in step with the seasons.

Common Traps Where students lose marks

Trap 1 — Day = rotation; year = revolution. Earth spinning on its axis gives the day; Earth going around the Sun gives the year.
Trap 2 — A month is ~29.5 days (Moon's phase cycle), not exactly 30.
Trap 3 — Shortest shadow = midday (Sun highest). Not sunrise or sunset.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. Which motion of the Earth gives us the day, and which the year?
Step 1:Earth's rotation on its axis → day (~24 h).
Step 2:Earth's revolution around the Sun → year (~365¼ days).
∴ Rotation = day; revolution = year.
Example B. When is a stick's shadow shortest, and what does it mark?
Step 1:The shadow is shortest when the Sun is highest (midday).
∴ The shortest shadow marks local noon — the basis of the mean solar day.
