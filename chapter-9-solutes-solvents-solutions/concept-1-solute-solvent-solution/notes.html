Solute, Solvent and Solution

A solution is a uniform mixture — the solute dissolves into the solvent and spreads evenly throughout.

Key Concept The three words to know

A solution is a uniform mixture (like salt or sugar in water — every sip tastes the same).

TermMeaning
SoluteThe substance that dissolves (e.g. salt, sugar)
SolventThe substance it dissolves in (e.g. water)
SolutionThe uniform mixture formed: Solute + Solvent → Solution
•When a solid dissolves in a liquid, the solid is the solute and the liquid is the solvent.
•When two liquids mix, the one present in the smaller amount is the solute, the one in the larger amount is the solvent.
Gulab jamun syrup: even though there's a lot of sugar in a little water, water is still the solvent and sugar the solute — because sugar is the one that dissolves.

Water dissolves so many substances that it is called a good (almost "universal") solvent. Salt and sugar dissolve in water but not in oil, because they dissolve only in certain solvents.

Common Traps Where students lose marks

Trap 1 — Solute dissolves; solvent does the dissolving. Salt (solute) dissolves in water (solvent).
Trap 2 — For two liquids, amount decides the role. Smaller amount = solute, larger = solvent — not which one "looks" like the liquid.
Trap 3 — A solution is uniform. Sand or chalk in water is non-uniform — not a true solution.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. In salt water, name the solute and the solvent.
Step 1:Salt dissolves → solute.
Step 2:Water does the dissolving → solvent.
∴ Solute = salt; Solvent = water.
Example B. A little vinegar (acetic acid) is mixed with a lot of water. Which is the solute?
Step 1:For two liquids, the smaller amount is the solute.
∴ Acetic acid (smaller amount) is the solute; water is the solvent.
