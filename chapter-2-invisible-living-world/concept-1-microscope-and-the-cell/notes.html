The Microscope and the Discovery of the Cell

The eye can only see things above a certain size. Lenses and the microscope opened up a hidden world.

Key Concept How we learnt to see the invisible

The human eye can only see objects above a certain size. Long ago, people found that a curved piece of glass — thick in the middle, thin at the edges, shaped like a lentil seed — could make small things look bigger. They called it a lens (from the word for lentil).

Activity 2.1: a round-bottomed flask full of water, placed over a book, makes the letters look larger — the water acts like a magnifying glass.

Two key scientists

ScientistYearContribution
Robert Hooke1665Book Micrographia; saw boxes in a slice of cork and named them cells — first use of the word "cell".
Antonie van Leeuwenhoek1660sMade better lenses; first to see bacteria and blood cells. Called the Father of Microbiology.

Magnification — how much bigger?

•Hooke's microscope: about 200–300 times.
•An ordinary light microscope: about 100–400 times.
•An electron microscope: about 10,00,000 (one million) times — shows tiny parts inside the cell.
Cell = basic unit of life. Hooke's word stuck: every living thing is made of one or more cells.

Common Traps Where students lose marks

Trap 1 — Who saw what. Hooke named the cell (from cork, which were dead, empty boxes). Leeuwenhoek first saw living microbes — he is the Father of Microbiology.
Trap 2 — Cork cells were not alive. Hooke saw empty compartments in dead cork; he did not see living cells.
Trap 3 — Magnification mix-up. Light microscope ≈ 100–400×; electron microscope ≈ 10,00,000×. Don't swap them.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. Why is Leeuwenhoek called the Father of Microbiology?
Step 1:He made improved, more powerful lenses/microscopes.
Step 2:He was first to clearly see and describe tiny living things such as bacteria.
∴ For first observing living microbes, he is called the Father of Microbiology.
Example B. Why is the word "cell" linked to a honeycomb?
Step 1:Hooke saw many small, empty, box-like spaces in cork.
Step 2:They reminded him of the compartments of a honeycomb, so he called each a "cell".
∴ The honeycomb-like compartments gave the cell its name.
