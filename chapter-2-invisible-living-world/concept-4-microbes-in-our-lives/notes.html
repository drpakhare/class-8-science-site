Microbes in Our Lives

Microbes make curd and bread, enrich soil, clean waste — and a few cause spoilage and disease.

Key Concept Friendly microbes at work

Most microbes are helpful. They touch nearly every part of our lives.

MicrobeWhat it does
Yeast (fungus)Respires and releases carbon dioxide, making dough soft and fluffy (bread, cakes); also a little alcohol → smell. Needs warmth & sugar.
Lactobacillus (bacterium)Turns milk into curd; produces lactic acid → sour taste. Works in warm conditions.
Rhizobium (bacterium)Lives in root nodules of legumes (peas, beans, lentils); traps nitrogen from air to enrich soil.
Bacteria & fungi in soilDecompose dead plants/animals into nutrient-rich manure; recycle nutrients.
Microalgae (e.g. Spirulina)Release oxygen (over half the Earth's O₂); food/health supplements; clean water; biofuel.
Idli, dosa, bhatura: made by fermentation — Lactobacillus and yeast at work. Biogas (methane + CO₂) is made by bacteria breaking down waste without oxygen.

A few harmful microbes

Some microbes spoil food (mould on fruit) and some cause disease in plants, animals and humans. Spoilage is prevented by drying, salting, adding sugar (pickles, murabba) or refrigeration — these stop microbes growing.

Why pickles don't spoil: high salt/sugar concentration does not let microbes grow on them — salt and sugar act as preservatives.

Common Traps Where students lose marks

Trap 1 — Yeast gives CO₂, Lactobacillus gives lactic acid. Yeast makes dough rise (gas); Lactobacillus makes curd sour (acid). Don't mix them up.
Trap 2 — Rhizobium fixes nitrogen, in legume root nodules. Not in cereals; that's why farmers rotate legumes with other crops.
Trap 3 — Decomposition vs manure. Microbes break complex dead matter into simpler nutrients (decomposition); the dark, nutrient-rich result is manure.
Preservation: salt/sugar/cold stop microbes growing — that is why curd forms in warm milk but not in the fridge.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. Why does dough with yeast rise and smell slightly different?
Step 1:Yeast respires, breaking down sugar and releasing carbon dioxide gas.
Step 2:CO₂ bubbles make the dough fluffy; a little alcohol is also formed.
∴ CO₂ makes it rise; the alcohol gives the smell.
Example B. Why do farmers grow legumes (peas/beans) in rotation with other crops?
Step 1:Legume root nodules contain Rhizobium bacteria.
Step 2:Rhizobium traps nitrogen from air and enriches the soil naturally.
∴ Rotation adds nitrogen to soil, so less chemical fertiliser is needed.
Example C. Why does milk turn to curd in a warm place but not in the fridge?
Step 1:Lactobacillus in curd ferments milk and forms lactic acid.
Step 2:These bacteria grow well in warmth, but cold slows them down.
∴ Warmth lets Lactobacillus act, so curd sets; cold prevents it.
