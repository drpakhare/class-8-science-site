Answer Key — Storms, Lightning and Cyclones

Final answer with a one-line reason. Full methods are in the Notes' worked examples.

Section A Objective

Q1
(b) thunderstorm — a thunderstorm has lightning and thunder.
Q2
(b) eye — the eye of the cyclone is calm and has the lowest pressure.
Q3
(b) warm ocean waters — warm ocean water supplies the moist rising air.
Q4
(c) crouch low in an open area — crouch low, away from tall objects; don't lie flat.
Q5
(a) Both true, R explains A — the rapid heating and expansion of air is exactly what makes thunder.
Q6
conductor — a lightning conductor protects buildings.
Q7
rotation — Earth's rotation makes the moving air spin.

Section B Short answer

Q8
Strong up/down winds rub water droplets and ice particles, building static charges; charges separate, and when very large the air's insulation breaks down, giving a sudden flow of charge — a flash of lightning.
Q9
A large spinning system of clouds, wind and rain around a very low-pressure centre; it forms over warm ocean waters.
Q10
Stay away from tall objects, crouch in a low open area (don't lie flat), avoid metal-rod umbrellas, get out of water, shelter in a car/bus (any three).

Section C Long / application

Q11
Warm moist air rises from the warm ocean; water vapour condenses releasing heat, warming the air more so it rises further, forming a very low-pressure centre. Surrounding air rushes in and Earth's rotation makes it spin. The eye is the lowest-pressure centre where wind is calm (strong winds revolve around it). Over land the warm-moist-air supply is cut off, so the cyclone weakens.
Q12
Warm moist air rises and condenses into towering clouds; up/down winds rub droplets and ice, separating charges. A huge charge breaks down air's insulation, giving lightning; the lightning heats air which expands rapidly, making thunder. A lightning conductor gives the charge an easy metal path to the ground, protecting the building.
