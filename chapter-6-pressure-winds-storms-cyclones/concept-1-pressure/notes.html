Pressure: Force per Unit Area

The same force feels very different over a sharp point or a broad strap — that difference is pressure.

Key Concept What pressure means

Pressure is the force acting per unit area, on the surface perpendicular to the force.

Pressure = Force ÷ Area   (P = F / A)
SI unit: newton per square metre (N/m²), also called the pascal (Pa). So 1 Pa = 1 N/m².

Same force, different pressure

•Smaller area → more pressure. A sharp nail tip or a knife's sharp edge concentrates force, so it cuts/pierces easily.
•Larger area → less pressure. Broad bag straps, a broad bucket handle, or a cloth pad under a head-load spread the weight, so they feel comfortable.

How to calculate

If a force of 100 N acts on an area of 2 m²: P = 100 ÷ 2 = 50 Pa.

Watch units: force in N, area in m², pressure in Pa. Keep them consistent.

Common Traps Where students lose marks

Trap 1 — Pressure depends on area, not just force. The same weight gives more pressure on a smaller area.
Trap 2 — Sharp = high pressure. A sharp edge/point has small area, so high pressure — that's why it cuts. Broad straps reduce pressure.
Trap 3 — Unit is pascal (Pa) = N/m². Don't confuse it with the newton (which is force).

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. A force of 60 N acts on an area of 3 m². Find the pressure.
Step 1:Use P = F / A.
Step 2:P = 60 ÷ 3 = 20.
∴ Pressure = 20 Pa (N/m²).
Example B. Why is it easier to cut with the sharp edge of a knife than the blunt edge?
Step 1:The sharp edge has a much smaller area of contact.
Step 2:For the same force, smaller area means greater pressure.
∴ Higher pressure under the sharp edge cuts more easily.
