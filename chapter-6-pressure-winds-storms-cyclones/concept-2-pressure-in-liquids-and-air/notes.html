Pressure in Liquids and Air

Liquids and air push in all directions — and the deeper you go, the greater the pressure.

Key Concept Liquids and air exert pressure

Pressure in liquids

•A liquid exerts pressure on the bottom and the sides of its container — in fact, in all directions.
•Liquid pressure depends on the height (depth) of the column — greater height, greater pressure. (It does not depend on the container's width.)
Everyday links: overhead tanks are placed high so taps get good pressure; a dam's base is broader because water pressure is largest near the bottom.

Pressure of air (atmospheric pressure)

The envelope of air around the Earth is the atmosphere. Air has weight and exerts atmospheric pressure on everything, in all directions.

•A rubber sucker sticks because outside air pressure is higher than the reduced pressure inside it.
•Atmospheric pressure is huge (the air column over a 15 cm × 15 cm area pushes with about 2250 N), yet we are not crushed because the pressure inside our body balances it.
Practical units: air pressure is often given in millibar (mb) or hectopascal (hPa); 1 mb = 1 hPa = 100 Pa.

Common Traps Where students lose marks

Trap 1 — Liquid pressure depends on height, not width. Two columns of the same height give the same pressure even if one is wider.
Trap 2 — Liquids/air press in ALL directions, not just downward.
Trap 3 — A sucker sticks due to outside air pressure, not "suction magic". The inside pressure is reduced.
Not crushed: our internal pressure equals atmospheric pressure, so it balances out.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. Why are overhead water tanks placed high up?
Step 1:Liquid pressure increases with the height of the column.
Step 2:A taller column gives more pressure at the taps.
∴ Height increases pressure, giving a strong stream from the taps.
Example B. Why does a rubber sucker stick to a smooth wall?
Step 1:Pressing it pushes air out, lowering the pressure inside.
Step 2:Higher outside atmospheric pressure holds it against the wall.
∴ The pressure difference makes the sucker stick.
