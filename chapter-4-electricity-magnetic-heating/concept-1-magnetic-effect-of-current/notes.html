The Magnetic Effect of Electric Current

A current-carrying wire deflects a compass needle — the discovery that electricity and magnetism are linked.

Key Concept Current makes a magnetic field

In Activity 4.1, a magnetic compass is placed under a wire. When the switch is ON (current flows), the compass needle deflects. When the switch is OFF, the needle returns to its original direction.

The big result: a current-carrying wire produces a magnetic field around it. This is the magnetic effect of electric current. The field disappears when the current stops.

Key words

•Magnetic field — the region around a magnet or current-carrying wire where its magnetic effect (e.g. a compass deflection) can be felt.
•The compass needle is itself a tiny magnet, so it responds to the wire's magnetic field.

Who discovered it?

Hans Christian Oersted (Denmark, 1820) noticed a compass needle deflect whenever a nearby circuit was switched on or off. He showed electricity and magnetism are linked — one of the great discoveries of science.

Uses: the magnetic effect runs electromagnets, electric bells, motors, fans and loudspeakers.

Common Traps Where students lose marks

Trap 1 — No current, no field. The magnetic field exists only while current flows; it vanishes when the current stops.
Trap 2 — It is current, not the wire, that matters. A plain wire with no current does not deflect the compass.
Trap 3 — Oersted, 1820. Remember the name and that the discovery is the link between electricity and magnetism.
Direction: reversing the current direction reverses the direction of the needle's deflection.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. Why does a compass needle deflect when current flows in a nearby wire?
Step 1:The current produces a magnetic field around the wire.
Step 2:The compass needle is a tiny magnet, so the field turns it.
∴ The current's magnetic field deflects the needle.
Example B. What happens to the needle when the current is switched off?
Step 1:No current → no magnetic field around the wire.
∴ The needle returns to its original (north–south) direction.
