Cells and Batteries

Inside a cell, a chemical reaction makes electricity — from Volta's first cell to today's rechargeable batteries.

Key Concept How a cell makes electricity

A cell (or battery) makes electric current from a chemical reaction inside it.

The Voltaic (Galvanic) cell

It has two electrodes (rods of two different metals) dipped in a liquid called an electrolyte (a weak acid or salt solution). The reaction between the metals and electrolyte produces electricity. Current flows from the positive terminal through the circuit to the negative terminal. When the chemicals are used up, the cell is "dead".

Lemon cell (Activity 4.6): copper wire + iron nail in lemons (lemon juice = electrolyte) can light an LED. Salt solution works too.

Dry cell

Called "dry" because the electrolyte is a moist paste, not a liquid. A zinc container is the negative terminal; a central carbon rod with a metal cap is the positive terminal. It is a single-use cell.

Rechargeable batteries

Can be charged and reused many times (phones, laptops, inverters, vehicles), saving money and reducing waste — but they slowly wear out. Most common type today: the lithium-ion battery.

Volta vs Galvani: Galvani saw a frog's leg twitch with two metals and thought electricity came from the frog; Volta showed it came from the metals + liquid, and built the first battery.

Common Traps Where students lose marks

Trap 1 — Electrodes must be two DIFFERENT metals. Same metals on both sides won't make a working cell.
Trap 2 — Electrolyte is a conducting liquid/paste. Pure water is a poor electrolyte; lemon juice or salt solution works. (In Fig 4.12, the lemon-juice cell lights the LED, the pure-water one does not.)
Trap 3 — "Dry" cell is not really dry. Its electrolyte is a moist paste; only the Voltaic cell uses a liquid electrolyte.
Dead cell: chemicals are used up, so it can't supply current; a dry cell is single-use, while rechargeables can be recharged.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. Name the parts of a Voltaic cell and what makes the electricity.
Step 1:Two electrodes of different metals dipped in an electrolyte (weak acid/salt solution).
Step 2:A chemical reaction between metals and electrolyte produces current.
∴ Electrodes + electrolyte; the chemical reaction makes the electricity.
Example B. In Fig 4.12, why does the LED glow with lemon juice but not with pure water?
Step 1:Lemon juice is a good electrolyte; pure water is not.
Step 2:Only with a working electrolyte does the chemical reaction drive a current.
∴ The lemon-juice cell makes current and lights the LED; the pure-water one does not.
